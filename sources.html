Sources & Citations

The framework presented on this site is a personal synthesis. It draws on established research across physics, biology, and systems theory, applying those principles to the architecture of inherited trauma and somatic holding patterns. Below are the primary scientific and theoretical sources that inform this work.

Physics & Geometry

The Quantum Corral Experiment

IBM Research / D.M. Eigler & E.K. Schweizer (1993)

Researchers positioned 48 iron atoms in a circle on a copper surface, creating a geometric boundary that forced the surface electrons into standing wave patterns. This experiment provides visual proof that geometric boundaries dictate wave behavior and frequency expression at the fundamental level.

View original paper in Nature

Cymatics: A Study of Wave Phenomena

Dr. Hans Jenny (1967)

The foundational documentation of how sound frequencies create visible, predictable geometric forms in matter (sand, water, plastics). Jenny's work demonstrates that frequency and geometry are inseparable expressions of the same underlying order.

Dissipative Structures

Ilya Prigogine (Nobel Prize in Chemistry, 1977)

Prigogine's work on open thermodynamic systems shows how complex systems maintain their organization by continuously exchanging energy with their environment. The framework applies this to human lineages: a family line is a dissipative structure that sustains itself by transforming energy, not conserving it unchanged.

Biology & Bioelectric Fields

Bioelectric Networks and Morphological Memory

Dr. Michael Levin, Tufts University

Levin's research demonstrates that bioelectric fields—an electrical information layer existing prior to and independent of genetic expression—dictate anatomical form. His work with planaria flatworms shows that changing the bioelectric field changes the physical structure, suggesting the body's "blueprint" is held in the field, not just the DNA.

Fascia as a Tensegrity Structure

Dr. Stephen Levin & Dr. Donald Ingber

Research into biotensegrity shows that the fascial network operates as a continuous, tension-dependent architectural system. It is piezoelectric, meaning it generates electrical charge in response to mechanical stress, effectively storing chronic tension and emotional bracing as physical architecture.

Neuroscience & Perception

Predictive Coding and the Brain

Dr. Karl Friston

The Free Energy Principle and predictive coding models show that the brain does not passively receive reality; it actively predicts it based on past experience (the "prior"), and only processes the prediction errors. This explains why cognitive approaches to trauma often fail: the nervous system filters incoming signals through the distortion of the old pattern before the conscious mind ever perceives them.
