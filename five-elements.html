The Five Elements of Pattern

The alphabet I used to build the Biogeometric Symbol Library. Every symbol on this site is a combination of these five forms.

Before there were symbols, there were shapes. And before there were shapes, there were rules — mathematical laws so fundamental that they appear in every culture, every tradition, and every living system on earth. The ancient Greeks called them the Platonic Solids. I call them the five elements of pattern: the irreducible forms from which all geometric complexity is built.

These are not decorations. They are the structural vocabulary I drew from when building the Biogeometric Symbol Library. Understanding them is not required to use the tools — but if you want to know why a symbol is built the way it is, this is where you start.

Tetrahedron
Fire
4 faces · 4 vertices · 6 edges

The simplest of the five — four equilateral triangles, the minimum number of faces needed to enclose three-dimensional space. The triangle is the most structurally stable shape in nature. Nothing can be added or removed without changing its fundamental character. This is why it maps to fire: it is pure, irreducible, and directional. It points.

In the Symbol Library The tetrahedron appears wherever a symbol needs to carry intention forward — focus, initiation, the spark of a new pattern. If you are beginning a clearing, this is the form you start with. It is the ignition geometry.

Hexahedron (Cube)
Earth
6 faces · 8 vertices · 12 edges

Six square faces, perfect right angles, equal in all directions. The cube is the geometry of physical matter — it is how crystals form, how cells stack, how the body organises its cellular architecture. It is the most stable of the five forms in the gravitational field. This is why it maps to earth: it holds, it grounds, it does not move unless moved.

In the Symbol Library The cube appears in symbols for grounding, physical body regulation, and cellular coherence. If the pattern you are working with lives in the body — in the fascia, the gut, the bones — the cube is the structural anchor. Start here if you need to feel safe in your own skin.

Octahedron
Air
8 faces · 6 vertices · 12 edges

Eight equilateral triangles, two pyramids joined at their base. The octahedron is the geometry of integration — it is simultaneously pointing up and pointing down, holding two directions in perfect balance. It is the bridge form. In crystal systems, it is the shape of diamond. In the body, it maps to the heart field and the respiratory system — the place where above and below meet.

In the Symbol Library The octahedron appears in symbols for integration, heart coherence, and the bridge between inherited pattern and present-moment choice. If you are working on the place where the old story meets the new one, this is the geometry that holds that threshold.

Dodecahedron
Ether
12 faces · 20 vertices · 30 edges

Twelve pentagonal faces — the most complex of the five, and the one Plato associated with the cosmos itself. The dodecahedron encodes the golden ratio (φ) in every proportion. It is the geometry of the field: the invisible structure that holds all other forms in relation to each other. It is the Listening Forest. It is the space in which all possibilities exist before any one of them is chosen.

In the Symbol Library The dodecahedron appears in symbols for ancestral field work, epigenetic clearing, and the recognition of inherited pattern. It is the form you use when you are working with something larger than one lifetime — when you are addressing the field itself, not just the symptom.

Icosahedron
Water
20 faces · 12 vertices · 30 edges

Twenty equilateral triangles — the most spherical of the five, the one that most closely approximates a circle in three dimensions. Water takes the shape of its container; the icosahedron is the geometry of that adaptability. It maps to the nervous system, emotional frequency, and the flow states that become available when the body is no longer braced against threat. It is the form of ease.

In the Symbol Library The icosahedron appears in symbols for nervous system regulation, emotional processing, and the restoration of flow after a period of contraction. If the pattern you are working with shows up as rigidity, shutdown, or the inability to feel safe in movement — this is the geometry that begins to soften it.
